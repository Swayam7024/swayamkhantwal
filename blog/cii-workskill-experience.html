My Experience at CII WorkSkill Competition

The Journey Begins

Competing at the CII Western Region WorkSkill Competition was one of the most challenging and rewarding experiences of my engineering journey. When I first heard about the competition, I knew I wanted to push myself to compete at the national level and demonstrate my capability in autonomous systems.

Preparation Phase

The three months leading up to the competition were intense. Our team focused on perfecting the autonomous drone delivery system that had been in development. We optimized every component:

  • Refined the Pixhawk flight controller configuration for maximum stability
  • Tested sensor fusion algorithms under various environmental conditions
  • Conducted multiple field tests of obstacle detection and avoidance
  • Prepared contingency plans for potential hardware failures
  • Created detailed documentation of the entire system architecture

The toughest challenge was ensuring that the system would perform flawlessly under competition pressure. We simulated different scenarios and stress-tested every component repeatedly.

Competition Day: The Challenge

The day of the competition arrived with mixed emotions—excitement, nervousness, and determination. The judging panel was composed of industry experts and academicians, which made the stakes even higher.

When our turn came, we demonstrated:

  • System Architecture: Explained the integration of Pixhawk, Raspberry Pi, and all supporting sensors
  • Autonomous Flight: Live demonstration of GPS waypoint navigation
  • Obstacle Avoidance: Real-time LiDAR-based obstacle detection
  • Failsafe Mechanisms: Showed how the system handles emergency situations
  • Problem-Solving: Discussed technical challenges and innovative solutions

Key Moments

There was a critical moment during the live demonstration when one of the sensors showed unusual behavior. Instead of panicking, our quick thinking and redundancy design saved the day. We switched to the backup sensor seamlessly, which impressed the judges and demonstrated real engineering maturity.

The judges appreciated not just the technical implementation but also our ability to explain complex concepts clearly and demonstrate practical problem-solving skills.

The Result: 2nd Position

When they announced that our team secured the 2nd position in the Industrial Electronics category, I felt a surge of pride and validation. This wasn't just about the award—it was recognition of months of hard work, learning, and innovation.

Lessons Learned

  • Preparation is paramount: The weeks of testing and optimization gave us the confidence to handle unexpected situations
  • Redundancy saves the day: Having backup systems and failsafes made the difference
  • Communication matters: Explaining technical concepts clearly to non-technical judges was as important as the implementation
  • Real-world testing beats theory: Field testing revealed issues that simulations couldn't predict
  • Innovation under pressure works: When you understand your system deeply, you can adapt on the fly

Moving Forward

This competition was a turning point in my engineering career. It validated my expertise in autonomous systems and gave me the confidence to tackle more complex problems. More importantly, it connected me with industry professionals and opened doors for internship and collaboration opportunities.

If you're thinking about competing in such events, my advice is: Start early, test continuously, and never underestimate the power of having a good team and backup plans.

The journey doesn't end here—it's just the beginning of a longer story in autonomous systems engineering.
